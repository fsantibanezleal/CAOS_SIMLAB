# 01 · Assumptions — S04 Emergency Department Patient Flow

The canonical instance, the modeling scope, and exactly what is and isn't represented. The math lives in
[02 · Formalization](./02_formalization.md); the engine in [03 · Solvers applied](./03_solvers-applied.md).

## The canonical instance — one ED shift

S04 models **a single emergency-department shift**: a finite stream of patients (`n_patients`, default
**240**) arriving over one shift, each traversing three stations in series —

> **triage → treatment → discharge**

— and leaving. The instance is deliberately small enough to run *live in the browser* (a few hundred
patients, a horizon of order `n / λ`), yet rich enough to exhibit every operational phenomenon the
scenario teaches: a bottleneck, a transient surge, priority, and per-class length-of-stay.

The default knobs (from the scenario's `param_specs`) frame the "typical shift":

| Knob | Symbol | Default | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| `lam` | λ | 2.0 | base arrival rate |
| `mu_triage` | μₜ | 3.0 | per-nurse triage service rate |
| `mu_treat` | μₓ | 0.8 | per-bay treatment service rate |
| `c_triage` | cₜ | 2 | triage nurses (the triage pool) |
| `c_treat` | cₓ | 3 | treatment bays (the treatment pool) |
| `p_urgent` | pᵤ | 0.25 | fraction of arrivals flagged urgent |
| `surge` | s | 0 | daytime-surge flag (0/1) |
| `n_patients` | n | 240 | patients in the shift |

The fixed discharge delay is a constant **d = 0.3** (time units), baked into the model rather than exposed
as a slider. Time units are abstract "shift time"; with μₓ = 0.8 a treatment episode averages ~1.25 units,
so the discharge tail is a small, deterministic add-on.

The ten shipped **variants** (see [04 · Results & reading](./04_results-and-reading.md)) sweep these knobs
around the typical shift: λ for load, cₓ for bay staffing, cₜ for the triage pool, pᵤ for the urgent mix,
and s for the surge.

## What IS modeled

- **Multi-stage flow under finite resources.** Three stations in series, each entity passing through all
  three; each station holds a queue and a limited pool of servers it must wait for.
- **Per-station queues.** Triage and treatment are both resource-limited, so a patient who finds every
  server busy *waits in line* at that station. The waiting — not a single best decision — is what S04
  measures.
- **Two-class priority at the bottleneck.** Treatment is a **priority** station: an **urgent** patient
  (priority code 0) jumps ahead of **standard** patients (code 1) in the treatment queue. Triage is plain
  FCFS — priority bites only where the bottleneck is.
- **Non-stationary arrivals.** The arrival intensity is time-varying: an optional **daytime surge** doubles
  the rate over the middle third-to-60% of the shift, modeling a mid-shift demand spike.
- **Per-class length-of-stay.** Total time-in-system is tracked separately for urgent vs standard patients,
  so the effect of priority (urgent LOS falls at the expense of standard LOS) is directly visible.

## What is NOT modeled (out of scope)

These are deliberate simplifications — listed so no one mistakes the educational model for an operational
one:

- **No abandonment / balking / reneging (LWBS).** Every arriving patient stays until discharged; nobody
  leaves the queue without being seen.
- **No preemption.** The treatment priority is **non-preemptive** (head-of-line): an urgent patient never
  interrupts a standard patient already in a bay — it only jumps the *waiting* line.
- **No dynamic re-prioritization.** A patient's class is fixed at arrival; it does not escalate while
  waiting.
- **No finite-capacity queues / blocking.** Queues are unbounded; there is no waiting-room cap that turns
  patients away.
- **No re-entry, no resources shared across stages.** Flow is strictly forward triage → treatment →
  discharge; triage nurses and treatment bays are disjoint pools.
- **No costs.** The model reports operational KPIs (LOS, wait, utilization), not money.
- **Discharge is a fixed delay, not a distribution.** d = 0.3 is deterministic — an ∞-server "hold," not a
  stochastic service.

## Stochastic & reproducibility assumptions

- **Markovian core.** Arrivals are Poisson (generated by thinning, below) and both service stages are
  **exponential** (memoryless). This makes the *core* Markovian — but the priority discipline and the
  non-stationary intensity push the full model **out of any simple closed form**, which is exactly why it
  is *simulated* rather than solved analytically (the lesson of the S01 → S04 ramp).
- **Non-stationary on purpose.** The system is **not** stationary: the intensity λ(t) is deliberately
  time-varying when the surge is on, so the model can show transient stress, not just a steady state.
- **Seeded / reproducible.** All variates — the candidate arrival epochs, the Bernoulli urgent flags, and
  both exponential service-time vectors — are **drawn up front** from a single seeded NumPy generator
  (`make_rng(seed)`) before the event loop runs. The same `(params, seed)` therefore reproduces *exactly*
  the same flow trace; this is the "replay = truth" contract that lets the static site replay rather than
  recompute.
- **Educational, synthetic-by-default.** The rates and mix are illustrative shift parameters, not data from
  any real hospital; the scenario is labeled educational and implies no clinical or service validity.
